Chronological History

How Social Media
Learned to Own You

From the first online community in 1985 to AI-generated feeds today — a decade-by-decade look at how platforms evolved their grip on human psychology.

1985
The Beginning

The WELL — First Online Community

The Whole Earth 'Lectronic Link launched in 1985, creating one of the first online communities. People paid per hour to discuss ideas, forming genuine connections. No algorithm. No ads. No engagement metrics. Just humans talking.

Early social spaces were pull — you chose what to read. Today's platforms are push — they decide for you.
1997
Birth of Social

Six Degrees — The First Social Network

SixDegrees.com let users create profiles and list friends — the first true social network. It reached 3.5 million users but shut down in 2001. Founder Andrew Weinreich later said the world "wasn't ready." He was two years early to a gold rush.

The architecture was already there. The addiction mechanisms weren't invented yet.
2004
The Tipping Point

Facebook Launches — and Changes the Game

Mark Zuckerberg launched "TheFacebook" from his Harvard dorm. Within a month, half of Harvard was on it. The key innovation wasn't the profile — it was the social graph. For the first time, your real identity was tied to your digital presence. Anonymity died.

Real identity = higher emotional stakes = more engagement. This was intentional.
2006
The Trap is Built

Infinite Scroll Invented by Aza Raskin

Working at a startup, Aza Raskin invented infinite scroll to make browsing smoother. He never considered the psychological consequences. By removing the natural break point of pagination, he eliminated the moment of conscious choice. You never decide to keep scrolling. You just do.

"I've spent many sleepless nights wondering whether I've made the world a worse place." — Aza Raskin, 2018
2009
Dopamine, Quantified

The Facebook Like Button Launches

Originally called the "Awesome" button, the Like was launched Feb 9, 2009. It was the moment social media became a validation machine. Every post became a bet: will people approve of me? The unpredictable reward of likes maps directly onto variable-ratio reinforcement — the same mechanism behind gambling addiction.

Former Facebook president Sean Parker admitted: "We gave you a little dopamine hit."
2011
Snapchat's Streak Trap

Snapchat Launches — and Invents FOMO Engineering

Snapchat's streak feature (added 2015) became one of the most psychologically powerful features ever built. Teens have missed family funerals and surgeries to maintain streaks. The feature exploits loss aversion: once a streak exists, losing it feels like a real loss — even if it never added value to begin with.

Snapchat's internal name for engagement: "Streaks and Scores." Pure behavioral design.
2016
The Algorithm Takes Over

Facebook & Instagram Kill the Chronological Feed

Until 2016, feeds showed posts in order. Then platforms switched to algorithmic curation — showing content predicted to generate the most engagement. The algorithm doesn't show you what's true, important, or healthy. It shows you what will make you react. Anger and outrage drive more engagement than joy. The algorithm learned this fast.

A Facebook internal study found that 64% of people who joined extremist groups were led there by the recommendation algorithm.
2018
The Whistleblower Era

Tristan Harris Testifies Before Congress

Former Google Design Ethicist Tristan Harris brought the concept of "persuasive technology" to mainstream consciousness. He described how every element of social media — from notification timing to interface color — is tested and optimized to maximize compulsive use. This testimony seeded the movement for tech regulation.

Harris coined the term "race to the bottom of the brain stem" — platforms compete to trigger the most primitive reactions.
2020
The For You Era

TikTok's Algorithm Redefines Addiction

TikTok's For You Page (FYP) is the most powerful recommendation engine ever deployed on consumers. It doesn't rely on your social graph. It studies your micro-behaviors — when you pause, rewatch, skip — and builds a psychological profile within minutes. New users report feeling "seen" by the algorithm. That feeling is engineered.

The average TikTok session is 10.85 minutes. The average attention span in 2000 was 12 seconds. In 2024: 8 seconds.
2021
The Facebook Papers

Frances Haugen Leaks Internal Research

Facebook whistleblower Frances Haugen leaked thousands of internal documents showing the company knew Instagram was harming teen girls' mental health, knew its algorithm amplified political extremism, and chose growth over safety at every turn. The documents showed internal research saying: "We make body image issues worse for one in three teen girls."

Facebook's internal motto was once "Move fast and break things." The things they broke included people.
2024
AI Takes the Wheel

Generative AI Enters the Feed

Meta, TikTok, and YouTube now deploy generative AI not just to recommend content — but to create it. AI-generated influencers, AI-written posts, AI-optimized thumbnails. The content you consume is increasingly made by machines trained to maximize your reaction. The line between organic and engineered human experience is gone.

You may already be having parasocial relationships with people who don't exist.
